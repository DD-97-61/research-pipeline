## Findings

### The Color of Status: Color Saturation, Brand Heritage, and Perceived Status of Luxury Brands
- **Authors:** Xinyue Zhou, Chunqu Xiao, Sunyee Yoon, Hong Zhu
- **Journal:** Journal of Consumer Research (JCR) | **Year:** 2026 | **Status:** published (Vol. 52, Issue 6, April 2026)
- **URL:** https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article/52/6/1232/8120421
- **Abstract summary:** The paper investigates how the color saturation of luxury brand products shapes consumers' perceptions of brand status. It argues that less saturated colors signal the passage of time and therefore a continuity-rich heritage, which in turn elevates perceived luxury status. The work sits squarely within the brand heritage / brand image literature.
- **Methodology:** Seven experimental and field studies (lab experiments plus at least one field study); abstract only — full text behind paywall.
- **Key findings:** Low (vs. high) color saturation increases luxury brand perceived status, mediated by perceived continuity heritage. The effect is mitigated when the brand emphasizes a recent founding year and is reversed when the brand positions itself as innovative. Saturation also influences willingness to pay and product choice.
- **Research gap signaled:** Boundary conditions around brand positioning (heritage vs. innovative) suggest further work is needed on how sensory/visual cues interact with dynamic repositioning, and on non-luxury categories where heritage signaling may work differently. (Inferred from abstract; full discussion section not accessed.)
- **Relevance:** ★★★★☆ — Directly targets brand heritage/image within a top UTD24 outlet; highly relevant to branding-core research program, especially visual identity and luxury positioning.

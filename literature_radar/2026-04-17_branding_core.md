## Verified new findings

### The Color of Status: Color Saturation, Brand Heritage, and Perceived Status of Luxury Brands
- **Authors:** Xinyue Zhou, Chunqu Xiao, Sunyee Yoon, Hong Zhu
- **Journal:** Journal of Consumer Research | **Year:** 2026 | **Status:** published (Vol. 52, Issue 6, April 2026 — DOI 10.1093/jcr/ucaf029)
- **URL:** https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article/52/6/1232/8120421
- **Abstract summary:** Investigates how the chromatic saturation of a luxury brand's visual assets feeds back into perceived status. Builds on a cognitive association between desaturated colors and the passage of time, which consumers read as continuity heritage — a known status cue for luxury brands.
- **Methodology:** Seven studies combining online experiments and field studies manipulating color saturation across logos, packaging, and product imagery; mediation tests for perceived continuity heritage; willingness-to-pay and product-choice outcomes.
- **Key findings:** Lower color saturation raises perceived brand status via continuity-heritage perceptions. The effect is attenuated when a brand emphasizes a recent founding year and reverses when the brand positions itself as innovative. Saturation shifts both WTP and product choice.
- **Research gap signaled:** Calls for work on how other sensory/aesthetic cues (typography, materials, soundscapes) co-construct heritage signals; whether the saturation–status link generalizes to non-luxury categories and to digital-native brands with no founding history to anchor on.
- **Relevance:** ★★★★☆ — Directly in the brand-image / brand-identity / luxury-heritage space and from a UTD24 outlet; less central to co-branding specifically, but highly citable for any paper touching on visual brand cues or heritage positioning.
